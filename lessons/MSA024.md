# Day 24 - Plurals Part 2: Introduction to Broken Plurals

## Lesson

1.  **What are Broken Plurals?**
    *   On Day 23, we learned about "Sound Plurals" because the original singular word mostly stayed "sound" (the same) and you just added an ending.
    *   **Broken Plurals** (`جَمْعُ التَّكْسِيرِ` - jam'u at-takseer) are different! Instead of just adding an ending, the **internal structure** of the singular word actually "breaks" or changes to form the plural.
    *   Think of English words like "man" becoming "men," "foot" becoming "feet," or "mouse" becoming "mice." You don't just add an "s" – the word itself changes. Arabic has many plurals like this.
    *   The challenging part is that there isn't a single, simple rule to know *how* a word will break. You often need to **memorize the broken plural along with its singular form**.

2.  **Introducing Common Broken Plurals**

    Let's learn some broken plurals for nouns you already know! Pay close attention to how the letters and vowels inside the word change.

    *   **Book:**
        *   Singular: `كِتَابٌ` (kitaabun) - a book (masculine)
        *   Plural: `كُتُبٌ` (kutubun) - books
            *   *Notice*: The long `ا` (Alif) is gone, and the short vowels change.
    *   **Pen:**
        *   Singular: `قَلَمٌ` (qalamun) - a pen (masculine)
        *   Plural: `أَقْلَامٌ` (aqlāmun) - pens
            *   *Notice*: An `أَ` (Alif-Fatha) is added at the beginning, and an `ا` (Alif) is added in the middle.
    *   **Door:**
        *   Singular: `بَابٌ` (baabun) - a door (masculine)
        *   Plural: `أَبْوَابٌ` (abwaabun) - doors
            *   *Notice*: Similar to `قَلَمٌ`, `أَ` is added at the beginning and `وَا` is added in the middle.
    *   **Desk / Office:**
        *   Singular: `مَكْتَبٌ` (maktabun) - a desk / an office (masculine)
        *   Plural: `مَكَاتِبُ` (makātibu) - desks / offices
            *   *Notice*: An `ا` (Alif) is added in the middle. Also, this plural ends with a single Damma (`ـُ`), not Tanween Damma (`ـٌ`). This is a special type of noun that doesn't take Tanween (you'll learn more about this later, for now, just observe the single Damma).
    *   **Star:**
        *   Singular: `نَجْمٌ` (najmun) - a star (masculine)
        *   Plural: `نُجُومٌ` (nujūmun) - stars
            *   *Notice*: A long `وُ` (Waw) is added in the middle.
    *   **Room:**
        *   Singular: `غُرْفَةٌ` (ghurfatun) - a room (feminine)
        *   Plural: `غُرَفٌ` (ghurafun) - rooms
            *   *Notice*: The `تَاء مَرْبُوطَة (ة)` is removed, and the vowels change. This shows that even feminine nouns can have broken plurals instead of the `ـَاتٌ` ending.

3.  **The Super Important Rule for Non-Human Broken Plurals!**
    *   This is one of the most important rules for broken plurals, especially for things (not people).
    *   In Arabic, **all non-human broken plural nouns are treated grammatically as if they are a single feminine item.**
    *   What does this mean?
        1.  **Adjectives** that describe a non-human broken plural must be in their **feminine singular form**.
            *   So, "new books" will be `كُتُبٌ جَدِيدَةٌ` (books new-feminine-singular), NOT `كُتُبٌ جَدِيدُونَ` or `كُتُبٌ جَدِيدَاتٌ`.
        2.  **Demonstrative Pronouns** ("this," "that") used to point to non-human broken plurals must be in their **feminine singular form**.
            *   So, to say "These are books," you would use `هَذِهِ` (this-feminine-singular) and say `هَذِهِ كُتُبٌ.` (This is books).
            *   To say "Those are cars" (if `سَيَّارَةٌ` had a broken plural instead of a sound plural), you would use `تِلْكَ` (that-feminine-singular). (Note: `سَيَّارَةٌ` actually has a sound plural `سَيَّارَاتٌ`, but the *rule of treating non-human plurals as feminine singular still applies to the adjective describing it and the demonstrative pronoun used with it for sound feminine plurals too! E.g. `هَذِهِ سَيَّارَاتٌ جَمِيلَةٌ` - "These are beautiful cars.")

    *   This rule often surprises new learners, but it is very consistent and you will get used to it!

## Examples

Let's put these new broken plurals and the "feminine singular agreement" rule into practice!

**1. Singular to Broken Plural Conversion:**
*   Singular: `كِتَابٌ` (a book)
    *   Plural: `كُتُبٌ` (books)
*   Singular: `قَلَمٌ` (a pen)
    *   Plural: `أَقْلَامٌ` (pens)
*   Singular: `غُرْفَةٌ` (a room)
    *   Plural: `غُرَفٌ` (rooms)

**2. Indefinite Broken Plural Nouns with Adjectives (Feminine Singular Adjective!):**
*   **New books.** (`كِتَابٌ` is masculine, but `كُتُبٌ` is a non-human broken plural)
    *   `كُتُبٌ جَدِيدَةٌ.` (kutubun jadeedatun.)
        *   *Notice*: `كُتُبٌ` is plural, but `جَدِيدَةٌ` (new) is feminine singular.
*   **Big doors.** (`بَابٌ` is masculine, but `أَبْوَابٌ` is a non-human broken plural)
    *   `أَبْوَابٌ كَبِيرَةٌ.` (abwaabun kabeeratun.)
        *   *Notice*: `أَبْوَابٌ` is plural, but `كَبِيرَةٌ` (big) is feminine singular.
*   **Small rooms.** (`غُرْفَةٌ` is feminine, `غُرَفٌ` is a non-human broken plural)
    *   `غُرَفٌ صَغِيرَةٌ.` (ghurafun sagheeratun.)
        *   *Notice*: `غُرَفٌ` is plural, and `صَغِيرَةٌ` (small) is feminine singular (it would have been feminine anyway, but this highlights the rule).

**3. Definite Broken Plural Nouns with Adjectives:**
*   **The beautiful stars.** (`نَجْمٌ` is masculine, but `نُجُومٌ` is a non-human broken plural)
    *   `النُّجُومُ الْجَمِيلَةُ.` (an-nujūmu al-jameelatu.)
        *   *Notice*: `النُّجُومُ` is definite plural, `الْجَمِيلَةُ` (beautiful) is definite feminine singular. (Remember Sun Letter `ن` for `النُّجُومُ`!)
*   **The big desks.** (`مَكْتَبٌ` is masculine, but `مَكَاتِبُ` is a non-human broken plural. Remember `مَكَاتِبُ` ends with single Damma.)
    *   `الْمَكَاتِبُ الْكَبِيرَةُ.` (al-makātibu al-kabeeratu.)
        *   *Notice*: `الْمَكَاتِبُ` is definite plural, `الْكَبِيرَةُ` (big) is definite feminine singular.

**4. Using Demonstrative Pronouns with Broken Plurals:**
*   **These are books.**
    *   `هَذِهِ كُتُبٌ.` (Hādhihi kutubun.)
        *   *Notice*: `هَذِهِ` (this-f.s.) is used for "these" books.
*   **These are new pens.**
    *   `هَذِهِ أَقْلَامٌ جَدِيدَةٌ.` (Hādhihi aqlāmun jadeedatun.)
        *   *Notice*: `هَذِهِ` for "these", and `جَدِيدَةٌ` (new) is feminine singular.
*   **Those are beautiful rooms.**
    *   `تِلْكَ غُرَفٌ جَمِيلَةٌ.` (Tilka ghurafun jameelatun.)
        *   *Notice*: `تِلْكَ` (that-f.s.) for "those" rooms, and `جَمِيلَةٌ` (beautiful) is feminine singular.

**5. Plurals in Sentences (Combining with prepositions):**
*   **The small pens are on the desks.**
    *   `الْأَقْلَامُ الصَّغِيرَةُ عَلَى الْمَكَاتِبِ.` (al-aqlāmu as-sagheeratu 'alā al-makātibi.)
        *   *Notice*: `الْأَقْلَامُ` is definite plural, `الصَّغِيرَةُ` is definite feminine singular. `عَلَى` makes `الْمَكَاتِبِ` (desks) end in Kasra (single Kasra, as it's a non-Tanween noun).
*   **The new doors are in the big rooms.**
    *   `الْأَبْوَابُ الْجَدِيدَةُ فِي الْغُرَفِ الْكَبِيرَةِ.` (al-abwaabu al-jadeedatu fī al-ghurafi al-kabeerati.)
        *   *Notice*: `الْأَبْوَابُ` (doors) is definite plural, `الْجَدِيدَةُ` (new) is definite feminine singular. `فِي` (in) makes `الْغُرَفِ الْكَبِيرَةِ` (the big rooms) end in Kasra (single Kasra, as it's a non-Tanween noun). Both `الْغُرَفِ` and `الْكَبِيرَةِ` are feminine singular in agreement.

This "feminine singular" rule for non-human plurals is a cornerstone of Arabic grammar, and you'll see it everywhere! It's a lot to take in, so don't worry if it feels challenging. The more you practice, the more natural it will become.
