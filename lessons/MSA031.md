# Day 31 - Relative Clauses

## Lesson

1.  **What are Relative Clauses?**
    *   Imagine you have a noun, like "the student." You can describe him with an adjective: "the **smart** student."
    *   But what if you want to say "the student **who studies in the school**"? The part "who studies in the school" is a **relative clause**. It acts like a long adjective, giving more information about the noun.
    *   In English, we often use words like "who," "which," or "that" to start these descriptive clauses. Arabic has special words for this too!

2.  **Introducing `الَّذِي` (alladhī) - "Who / Which / That" (Masculine Singular)**
    *   This word is used when the noun you are describing is **definite** (has `الْـ`), **masculine**, and **singular**.
    *   **Structure:** `الْـ` [Masculine Singular Noun] + `الَّذِي` + [Descriptive Clause]
    *   **Pronunciation hint:** 'all-la-dhee', with a long 'ee' sound. The `ل` (Laam) has a Shaddah.

3.  **Introducing `الَّتِي` (allatī) - "Who / Which / That" (Feminine Singular)**
    *   This word is used when the noun you are describing is **definite** (has `الْـ`), **feminine**, and **singular**.
    *   **Structure:** `الْـ` [Feminine Singular Noun] + `الَّتِي` + [Descriptive Clause]
    *   **Pronunciation hint:** 'all-la-tee', with a long 'ee' sound. The `ل` (Laam) has a Shaddah.

4.  **How the Descriptive Clause Works (The "Return" to the Noun)**
    *   The sentence or phrase that comes *after* `الَّذِي` or `الَّتِي` must contain something that refers back to the noun you are describing. Think of it as a "return ticket" to the original noun.
    *   For beginners, we'll look at two simple ways this "return" works:

    *   **A) The Clause is a Verb, and the Subject is the Noun:**
        *   If you say `الرَّجُلُ الَّذِي كَتَبَ.`, it means "The man who wrote."
        *   `كَتَبَ` (he wrote) already has "he" inside it. That "he" refers back to `الرَّجُلُ` (the man). It's an implicit (understood) return.
        *   So, `الَّذِي` connects `الرَّجُلُ` to `كَتَبَ`.

    *   **B) The Clause is a Location (Prepositional Phrase), describing "where the noun is":**
        *   If you say `الْكِتَابُ الَّذِي فَوْقَ الْمَكْتَبِ.`, it means "The book that is on the desk."
        *   In Arabic, you don't need a word for "is" in this kind of clause. The location `فَوْقَ الْمَكْتَبِ` directly describes *where the book is*, linking back to `الْكِتَابُ` through `الَّذِي`.
        *   The word for "is" (or "are") is implied.

    *   **Important:** The noun you are describing *must* be **definite** (have `الْـ`) to use `الَّذِي` or `الَّتِي`. If it's indefinite (e.g., `كِتَابٌ`), you don't use `الَّذِي`/`الَّتِي`; you just put the descriptive clause right after it, but we won't focus on that today.

This is a powerful new way to add detail to your sentences!

## Examples

Let's put `الَّذِي` and `الَّتِي` into practice with nouns and verbs/locations you already know.

**1. Describing a masculine singular noun (`الَّذِي`):**

*   **The student who wrote.**
    *   `الطَّالِبُ الَّذِي كَتَبَ.` (Aṭ-ṭālibu alladhī kataba.)
        *   `الطَّالِبُ` (The student) is definite, masculine, singular. `الَّذِي` links to `كَتَبَ` (he wrote), where "he" refers to "the student."

*   **The teacher who studies in the university.**
    *   `الْمُدَرِّسُ الَّذِي يَدْرُسُ فِي الْجَامِعَةِ.` (Al-mudarrisu alladhī yadrusu fī al-jāmi'ati.)
        *   `الْمُدَرِّسُ` (The teacher) is definite, masculine, singular. `الَّذِي` links to `يَدْرُسُ` (he studies), where "he" refers to "the teacher."

*   **The book that is on the desk.**
    *   `الْكِتَابُ الَّذِي فَوْقَ الْمَكْتَبِ.` (Al-kitābu alladhī fawqa al-maktabi.)
        *   `الْكِتَابُ` (The book) is definite, masculine, singular. `الَّذِي` links to `فَوْقَ الْمَكْتَبِ` (on the desk), describing the book's location.

*   **The pen that is in my room.**
    *   `الْقَلَمُ الَّذِي فِي غُرْفَتِي.` (Al-qalamu alladhī fī ghurfatī.)
        *   `الْقَلَمُ` (The pen) is definite, masculine, singular. `الَّذِي` links to `فِي غُرْفَتِي` (in my room), describing the pen's location.

**2. Describing a feminine singular noun (`الَّتِي`):**

*   **The student who studied.**
    *   `الطَّالِبَةُ الَّتِي دَرَسَتْ.` (Aṭ-ṭālibatū allatī darasat.)
        *   `الطَّالِبَةُ` (The student) is definite, feminine, singular. `الَّتِي` links to `دَرَسَتْ` (she studied), where "she" refers to "the student."

*   **The teacher who writes the story.**
    *   `الْمُدَرِّسَةُ الَّتِي تَكْتُبُ الْقِصَّةَ.` (Al-mudarrisatū allatī taktubu al-qisṣata.)
        *   `الْمُدَرِّسَةُ` (The teacher) is definite, feminine, singular. `الَّتِي` links to `تَكْتُبُ` (she writes), where "she" refers to "the teacher."

*   **The car that is under the house.**
    *   `السَّيَّارَةُ الَّتِي تَحْتَ الْبَيْتِ.` (As-sayyāratu allatī taḥta al-bayti.)
        *   `السَّيَّارَةُ` (The car) is definite, feminine, singular. `الَّتِي` links to `تَحْتَ الْبَيْتِ` (under the house), describing the car's location.

*   **The school that is in the city.** (Let's use `مَدِينَةٌ` (madīnatun) = city for a new word, it's feminine).
    *   `الْمَدْرَسَةُ الَّتِي فِي الْمَدِينَةِ.` (Al-madrasatu allatī fī al-madīnati.)
        *   `الْمَدْرَسَةُ` (The school) is definite, feminine, singular. `الَّتِي` links to `فِي الْمَدِينَةِ` (in the city), describing the school's location.

You can now use a whole descriptive clause to give more detail about a specific noun! This makes your sentences much richer and more precise.

Let's rephrase for simplicity for a first grader: `الْقِصَّةُ الَّتِي دَرَسَتْهَا.` (The story that she studied.) This uses an attached pronoun, so let's use a simpler example.

## 📚 Core Vocabulary
*   الَّذِي (alladhī) - who, which, that (for definite masculine singular)
*   الَّتِي (allatī) - who, which, that (for definite feminine singular)
*   مَدِينَةٌ (madīnatun) - a city
