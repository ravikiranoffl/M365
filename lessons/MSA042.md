Today is a very exciting day! We're going to learn about **Active and Passive Participles**. These are special words that come from verbs but act like adjectives or nouns. They help us describe *who is doing* an action or *what has had an action done to it*. It's like turning "write" into "writer" or "written thing"!

# Day 42 - Active and passive participles

## Lesson

1.  **What are Participles?**
    *   Think of participles as words that share qualities of both verbs and adjectives.
    *   An **Active Participle** describes the *doer* of an action. For example, from "to write," we get "writer" or "writing (person)."
    *   A **Passive Participle** describes the *receiver* of an action, or something *that has been acted upon*. For example, from "to write," we get "written (thing)."
    *   We'll focus on forming these from the simplest kind of verb, the 3-letter verbs you've mostly been learning (like `كَتَبَ` and `دَرَسَ`).

2.  **The Active Participle (اسم الفاعل - ism al-fāʿil)**
    *   This pattern describes the **person or thing doing the action**.
    *   It follows a pattern: `فَاعِلٌ` (fāʿilun).
    *   To make it, you generally take the three root letters of a verb, add an `ا` (Alif) after the first letter, and give the second root letter a Kasra ( ِ ). The ending will usually be Tanween Damma ( ٌ ) for indefinite masculine singular.
    *   Let's see some examples from verbs you know:
        *   **Root: `ك ت ب` (k-t-b) - "to write"**
            *   Verb: `كَتَبَ` (kataba) - he wrote
            *   Active Participle: `كَاتِبٌ` (kātibun)
                *   *Meaning:* "a writer" or "writing (masculine singular)"
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'kaa-ti-bun' (long 'aa', short 'i', 'bun' for Tanween Damma).
        *   **Root: `د ر س` (d-r-s) - "to study"**
            *   Verb: `دَرَسَ` (darasa) - he studied
            *   Active Participle: `دَارِسٌ` (dārisun)
                *   *Meaning:* "a student" or "studying (masculine singular)" (You already know `طَالِبٌ` for student, which is also an active participle from a different root!)
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'daa-ri-sun'.
        *   **Root: `ذ ه ب` (dh-h-b) - "to go"**
            *   Verb: `ذَهَبَ` (dhahaba) - he went
            *   Active Participle: `ذَاهِبٌ` (dhāhibun)
                *   *Meaning:* "going (masculine singular)"
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'dhā-hi-bun'.
        *   **Root: `أ ك ل` (a-k-l) - "to eat"**
            *   Verb: `أَكَلَ` (akala) - he ate
            *   Active Participle: `آكِلٌ` (ākilun)
                *   *Meaning:* "an eater" or "eating (masculine singular)" (Remember how `أَاكُلُ` becomes `آكُلُ`? Similar here: `أَاكِلٌ` becomes `آكِلٌ`).
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'aa-ki-lun'.
        *   **Root: `ش ر ب` (sh-r-b) - "to drink"**
            *   Verb: `شَرِبَ` (shariba) - he drank
            *   Active Participle: `شَارِبٌ` (shāribun)
                *   *Meaning:* "a drinker" or "drinking (masculine singular)"
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'shaa-ri-bun'.
        *   **Root: `ر ج ع` (r-j-') - "to return"**
            *   Verb: `رَجَعَ` (raja'a) - he returned
            *   Active Participle: `رَاجِعٌ` (rāji'un)
                *   *Meaning:* "returning (masculine singular)"
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'raa-ji-'un'.

    *   **Gender and Number:** Like regular adjectives, these participles can change their endings to match the noun they describe in gender, definiteness, and case.
        *   `كَاتِبٌ` (kātibun) - a writer (m.s.)
        *   `كَاتِبَةٌ` (kātibatun) - a writer (f.s.) (add `ـَةٌ`)
        *   `الْكَاتِبُ` (al-kātibu) - the writer (definite m.s.)
        *   `الْكَاتِبَةُ` (al-kātibatu) - the writer (definite f.s.)

3.  **The Passive Participle (اسم المفعول - ism al-mafʿūl)**
    *   This pattern describes the **person or thing that has received the action**.
    *   It follows a pattern: `مَفْعُولٌ` (mafʿūlun).
    *   To make it, you generally add `مُـ` (mu-) to the beginning, a `و` (Waw) before the last letter, and give the first root letter a Sukoon. The ending will usually be Tanween Damma ( ٌ ) for indefinite masculine singular.
    *   Let's see some examples:
        *   **Root: `ك ت ب` (k-t-b) - "to write"**
            *   Verb: `كَتَبَ` (kataba) - he wrote
            *   Passive Participle: `مَكْتُوبٌ` (maktūbun)
                *   *Meaning:* "written (masculine singular)"
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'mak-too-bun' (short 'a', Sukoon on `ك`, long 'oo', 'bun').
        *   **Root: `د ر س` (d-r-s) - "to study"**
            *   Verb: `دَرَسَ` (darasa) - he studied
            *   Passive Participle: `مَدْرُوسٌ` (madrūsun)
                *   *Meaning:* "studied (masculine singular)"
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'mad-roo-sun'.
        *   **Root: `أ ك ل` (a-k-l) - "to eat"**
            *   Verb: `أَكَلَ` (akala) - he ate
            *   Passive Participle: `مَأْكُولٌ` (ma'kūlun)
                *   *Meaning:* "eaten (masculine singular)"
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'ma'-koo-lun'.
        *   **Root: `ش ر ب` (sh-r-b) - "to drink"**
            *   Verb: `شَرِبَ` (shariba) - he drank
            *   Passive Participle: `مَشْرُوبٌ` (mashrūbun)
                *   *Meaning:* "drunk (masculine singular)" (often used as a noun for "a drink" or "a beverage")
                *   *Pronunciation hint:* 'mash-roo-bun'.

    *   **Gender and Number:** Like active participles, passive participles also change for gender, definiteness, and case.
        *   `مَكْتُوبٌ` (maktūbun) - written (m.s.)
        *   `مَكْتُوبَةٌ` (maktūbatun) - written (f.s.) (add `ـَةٌ`)
        *   `الْمَكْتُوبُ` (al-maktūbu) - the written (definite m.s.)
        *   `الْمَكْتُوبَةُ` (al-maktūbatu) - the written (definite f.s.)

This is a very powerful way to create new adjectives and nouns from verbs, adding a lot of flexibility to your Arabic!

## Examples

Let's use these active and passive participles in sentences. Remember they act like adjectives, so they'll follow the noun in definiteness, gender, and case!

1.  **The writing student is in the school.** (meaning: the student who writes/is writing)
    *   `الطَّالِبُ الْكَاتِبُ فِي الْمَدْرَسَةِ.` (Aṭ-ṭālibu al-kātibu fī al-madrasati.)
        *   *Notice*: `الطَّالِبُ` (the student) is definite, masculine, singular, Damma. So `الْكَاتِبُ` (the writing one) matches it perfectly.

2.  **The returning teacher (masculine) is here.**
    *   `الْمُدَرِّسُ الرَّاجِعُ هُنَا.` (Al-mudarrisu ar-rāji'u hunā.)

3.  **She is a going student (feminine).** (Meaning: a student who is going)
    *   `هِيَ طَالِبَةٌ ذَاهِبَةٌ.` (Hiya ṭālibatun dhāhibatun.)
        *   *Notice*: `طَالِبَةٌ` is indefinite, feminine, singular, Tanween Damma. So `ذَاهِبَةٌ` matches.

4.  **This is a written book.**
    *   `هَذَا كِتَابٌ مَكْتُوبٌ.` (Hādhā kitābun maktūbun.)
        *   *Notice*: `كِتَابٌ` is indefinite, masculine, singular, Tanween Damma. So `مَكْتُوبٌ` matches.

5.  **The studied story is new.** (meaning: the story that has been studied)
    *   `الْقِصَّةُ الْمَدْرُوسَةُ جَدِيدَةٌ.` (Al-qisṣatu al-madrūsatū jadeedatun.)
        *   *Notice*: `الْقِصَّةُ` (the story) is definite, feminine, singular, Damma. So `الْمَدْرُوسَةُ` (the studied one) matches (added `ـَةُ` for feminine).

6.  **The eaten food is big.**
    *   `الطَّعَامُ الْمَأْكُولُ كَبِيرٌ.` (Aṭ-ṭa'āmu al-ma'kūlu kabeerun.)

7.  **Is this a drink?** (using `مَشْرُوبٌ` as a noun for 'drink/beverage')
    *   `هَلْ هَذَا مَشْرُوبٌ؟` (Hal hādhā mashrūbun?)

8.  **I am a student, an eater and a drinker.**
    *   `أَنَا طَالِبٌ وَ آكِلٌ وَ شَارِبٌ.` (Ana ṭālibun wa ākilun wa shāribun.)

You can now use these special verb-adjectives to describe things in a very concise way!

8.  Translate: "This is a going car." (`سَيَّارَةٌ`)
